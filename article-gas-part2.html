2. Middle Layer Savings (Third-Party Rebates)

This slice is smaller but still meaningful. Apps like Upside bring additional discounts. I earned $26.76 on my primary Upside account, $24.68 on my secondary, and $10.99 on my tertiary, for a total of $62.43 cashed out, with another $3.44 pending since Upside requires a $10 minimum.

That's real money layered directly onto my fuel purchases. Upside even used to give a $0.50-per-gallon sign-up bonus (until people abused it—sorry, y'all). Once the app bonuses hit, the final layer of my strategy comes from the cards in my wallet.

3. Back-End Savings (Cashback Cards)

My final layer is the one most people forget: credit card cashback. My Fidelity Visa earns a flat 2% year-round. My Discover It offered 5% cashback on gas and other transit necessities during the third quarter, which spans from July through September.

I earned $14.84 during the quarter and estimate an additional $26.66 from Fidelity across the rest of the year, totaling about $41.50. Because Discover matches your first year's rewards, that number jumps to $56.34 overall.

Even accounting for a ±$5.63 margin of error due to some tracking issues across cards, that's still at least $50 in credit card rewards just for fueling up as usual. Eventually, I'd like to get a dedicated fuel and transit card, but that's an article for another day.

The Big Picture

So what does all this mean? In a single year of tracking, I saved 25% compared to the state average on prices at the pump alone, along with:

  • $65.87 in app rebates.
  • Added another $50+ in credit card rewards.
  • And grabbed $50+ in loyalty discounts not applied at the pump.

Add it up, and that's roughly $165+ in rewards and $500 in under-market fuel costs, a total benefit of over $600 from simply paying attention.

Even if you don't drive as much as I do, and you probably don't, $100 in savings can still grow into more than $5,000 when put to work, just like we talked about in the 25–500 Plan. It's proof that Benjamin Franklin's old saying, "a penny saved is a penny earned," still holds today—only now it's measured in thousands instead of cents.
