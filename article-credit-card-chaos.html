We've all heard it. Samuel L. Jackson or Jennifer Garner asking, "What's in your wallet?" on TV or YouTube. It's a catchy Capital One slogan, but I take it seriously because choosing the right cards literally puts money back in my pocket. Here's what's in mine:

  • The Discover It cashback card
  • Debit cards from PNC and Bank of America
  • A Fidelity CMA debit card
  • A Circle K gas card for fuel discounts
  • The Fidelity 2% Signature Visa
  • And now, the PayPal cashback debit card

That may sound like overkill, but once you understand how this system works, you start to realize just how much money is on the table.

Every swipe of a card kicks off a chain of events. Merchants pay a fee, typically 2 to 3 percent, to accept credit cards, and to a lesser extent, debit cards as well. The card network (like Visa or Discover), the issuing bank, and a few back-end processors split that fee. With Discover, it's even simpler. They issue the card, lending me money directly, and run the network, so they keep both cuts. Then they hand me 1% cash back for using it, or 5% on a rotating quarterly category.

It adds up. Today at Lydia's Coffeehouse in New Hampshire (great Americano by the way!), I saw a sign that said they spend over $700 a month on credit card fees. And still, no surcharge, no minimum. That's how ubiquitous cards have become. A recent Wall Street Journal article noted that massive corporations, such as Walmart and Amazon, are developing their own cryptocurrencies to bypass the billions they pay in fees each year. Small shops, especially, can't afford to go cash-only, and they certainly don't have the resources to launch their own cryptocurrency. For now, businesses and consumers are stuck between the high fees of credit card networks and the lack of competitive payment alternatives, forced to play by the card companies' rules and hoping to come out ahead.

It's a messy and convoluted system, but as consumers, we can use it to our advantage. Understanding this complex system is just the first step.
